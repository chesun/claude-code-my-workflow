**Literature Review & Positioning Memo**

Immigration Enforcement, Education, and Long-Run Labor Market Outcomes

*Proposed Study: Swift/Cactus Raid (December 2006) -- TERC/TWC Linked
Individual Data*

1\. Purpose

This memo classifies all relevant published literature on immigration
enforcement and education/labor market outcomes, separating papers by
(a) whether identification is causal, (b) disciplinary field, and (c)
the specific enforcement variation exploited. The goal is to precisely
position a proposed study of the December 2006 Swift/Cactus workplace
raid using TERC individual student data linked to TWC wage records,
relative to the existing causal economics literature.

2\. Classification Criteria

**Causal (Economics):** Paper published in an economics journal (or by
economists in a policy journal), using a credible identification
strategy (DiD, staggered DiD, IV, RDD, synthetic control, event study)
with variation in enforcement exposure. Shaded **blue** in the table
below.

**Causal (Other Field):** Paper using a plausibly causal design but
published in education, sociology, public health, or policy journals,
typically by non-economists. Shaded **yellow** in the table below.

**Non-Causal / Descriptive:** Correlational, qualitative, or descriptive
work (OLS with fixed effects but no quasi-experimental variation;
survey-based; case studies). Shaded **gray** in the table below.

4\. Positioning Relative to Causal Economics Literature

4A. What the Causal Economics Literature Has Established

The causal economics literature on immigration enforcement and human
capital has converged on several findings, all measured over short time
horizons (typically 1--4 years post-enforcement):

-   Enforcement reduces school enrollment for Hispanic students (Dee &
    Murphy 2020, exploiting staggered 287(g)/SC adoption).

-   Enforcement lowers test scores for Hispanic and Black students
    (Bellows 2019; Bennett et al. 2025, exploiting staggered SC and FOIA
    arrest records).

-   Enforcement increases school dropout and grade retention among
    children of likely unauthorized parents (Amuedo-Dorantes & Lopez
    2015, exploiting MSA-level enforcement intensity variation).

-   Enforcement increases child poverty in mixed-status households
    (Amuedo-Dorantes et al. 2018, exploiting enforcement intensity
    index).

-   Enforcement reduces employment and wages for both immigrants and
    US-born workers in targeted labor markets (East et al. 2023,
    exploiting staggered SC).

-   Enforcement worsens birth outcomes for Latina mothers (Tomé et al.
    2021; Novak et al. 2017).

4B. What the Literature Has Not Established

Despite the breadth of this literature, four critical gaps remain:

**1. No study traces childhood enforcement exposure to adult labor
market outcomes.** Every causal paper above measures short-run effects
(test scores, enrollment, attendance, poverty, wages of current
workers). No paper follows exposed children into adulthood to measure
whether enforcement-induced educational disruption translates into
permanently lower earnings, reduced employment, or downward occupational
mobility. East et al. (2023) study contemporaneous adult labor markets;
they do not track the children affected by enforcement.

**2. No study uses individual-level linked education-to-employment
administrative data.** The causal economics papers rely on ACS survey
data (Amuedo-Dorantes; East et al.; Bansak et al.), SEDA district
aggregates (Bellows; Weber), district enrollment counts (Dee & Murphy),
or birth certificates (Tomé et al.; Novak et al.). The non-economics
papers use school-level TEA data (Avila), county aggregates of
TEA/THECB/TWC (Kirksey & Sattin-Bajaj 2025), or linked health-education
admin data for short-run outcomes (Heinrich et al. 2023). No paper links
individual student records through the entire education pipeline into
individual wage records.

**3. No study examines the 2006 Swift/Cactus raid---the largest
workplace enforcement action of its era.** The entire causal
workplace-raid literature examines Trump-era operations (Load Trail
2018, Morristown 2018). The Bush-era Swift raids (Operation Wagon Train,
December 12, 2006), which arrested \~1,300 workers across six states and
devastated Cactus, TX, have never been studied with any form of
systematic quantitative data. Only journalistic and descriptive accounts
exist (CIS 2009, Texas Tribune 2018, Texas Monthly 2025).

**4. No study provides long-run outcome measurement.** The longest
follow-up in the causal economics literature is approximately 4--5 years
(Bellows 2019 tracks SC counties through test score data ending around
2013--2014). Kirksey & Sattin-Bajaj (2025, education, not economics)
extend to \~5 post-raid cohorts for county-level postsecondary outcomes.
A study of the 2006 Swift/Cactus raid would permit 17+ years of
post-event observation---long enough to track kindergartners at the time
of the raid through high school completion, college enrollment, and
early-career earnings.

5\. Proposed Contribution

The proposed study would fill all four gaps simultaneously by exploiting
the December 12, 2006 Swift/Cactus raid as a natural experiment, using
TERC individual student records (1990--2024+) linked to TWC quarterly
wage records. This positions the paper as follows:

Relative to the Causal Economics Literature:

-   Extends the enforcement-education literature (Bellows 2019;
    Amuedo-Dorantes & Lopez 2015; Dee & Murphy 2020; Bennett et
    al. 2025) from short-run school outcomes to long-run labor market
    outcomes, answering: do the test score declines, enrollment losses,
    and dropout increases documented in those papers translate into
    permanently lower earnings?

-   Complements the enforcement-labor market literature (East et al.
    2023; Bansak et al. 2024) by studying a fundamentally different
    channel: not how enforcement reshapes contemporaneous labor markets,
    but how childhood exposure to enforcement shapes the next
    generation\'s human capital accumulation and earnings trajectories.

-   Upgrades data from survey-based (ACS) and district-aggregate to
    individual-level linked administrative records (TERC student records
    → TWC wage records), eliminating measurement error from
    self-reported outcomes and enabling within-student tracking across
    the full education-to-labor-market pipeline.

Relative to the Education / Sociology Literature:

-   Directly addresses the call in Kirksey & Sattin-Bajaj (2025) for
    \"longitudinal analyses that track students over extended periods\"
    to \"capture delayed impacts not observable in shorter-term,
    immediate post-raid studies.\" Our study does exactly this, but with
    individual-level data rather than county aggregates, and over a much
    longer horizon.

-   First quantitative study of the Swift/Cactus 2006 raid. Avila
    (2024), Kirksey & Sattin-Bajaj (2023, 2025), and Heinrich et
    al. (2023) all study Trump-era raids. The Bush-era workplace
    enforcement regime---which pioneered mass raids as a deterrence
    strategy---has no comparable empirical evaluation.

Core Novelty (Economics Positioning):

The paper's primary contribution to economics is answering a question
that the existing causal literature has established the preconditions
for but cannot itself answer: Does childhood exposure to immigration
enforcement cause permanently lower adult earnings? The
enforcement-education papers show short-run educational harm. The
enforcement-labor market papers show contemporaneous market disruption.
But no paper connects these two channels through the life course of
affected children. The 2006 Swift/Cactus raid, combined with 17+ years
of individual-level TERC-TWC linked data, makes this possible for the
first time.

7\. Risks and Limitations

-   Single-event case study (N=1 treated location): Limits external
    validity. However, the workplace-raid literature in both economics
    and education is built on case studies (Heinrich et al. 2023:
    Morristown; Kirksey & Sattin-Bajaj 2023, 2025: Load Trail; Avila
    2024: Load Trail; Novak et al. 2017: Postville). The length of
    follow-up and individual linkage would differentiate this paper even
    within the case-study paradigm.

-   Small treated population: Cactus, TX had \~3,000 residents. The
    number of school-age children in the TERC data within Moore County
    (and surrounding counties like Oldham, Hartley, Sherman, Hutchinson,
    Dallam) will determine statistical power. This is a feasibility
    question that must be checked against the data before proceeding.

-   Compositional changes: If affected families left the area after the
    raid (as journalistic accounts suggest), the remaining TERC-observed
    students may not be the most directly affected. This is the standard
    selective attrition concern in enforcement studies and can be
    partially addressed through intent-to-treat framing and mobility
    analysis.

-   Confounding events over 17-year window: The 2008 financial crisis,
    Great Recession, SC rollout, and COVID-19 all occurred during the
    follow-up period. A synthetic control or matched-cohort design with
    students in comparable Panhandle communities can absorb common
    shocks, but unmeasured local confounders remain a concern.
